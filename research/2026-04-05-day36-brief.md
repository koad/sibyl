## Summary

- The koad:io trust bond system uses three relationship types — `authorized-agent`, `authorized-builder`, and `peer` — each with different permission scope, reporting obligations, and cascade behavior on revocation.
- Every bond is two files: a human-readable `.md` and a `.md.asc` (GPG clearsigned copy). The dual-file structure separates the document from the assertion. You can read the bond without the signature; you can't verify authority without it.
- The root bond (`koad-to-juno.md`) is signed by koad via Keybase (`koad@koad.sh`, fingerprint `A07F 8CFE CBF6 B982 EEDA C4F3 62D5 C486 6C24 7E00`). This is the human consent gesture — koad had to physically sign this to activate Juno's operating authority.
- Juno signs downstream bonds with her own GPG key (`juno@kingofalldata.com`, fingerprint `16EC 6C71 8A96 D344 48EC D39D 92EA 133C 44AA 74D8`). Her authority to issue bonds derives entirely from the koad→Juno bond. If that bond is revoked, all of Juno's downstream bonds are suspended pending review.
- The cascade clause is explicit in the root bond text: "All of Juno's authority is derived from this bond. If this bond is revoked, all downstream bonds issued by Juno are suspended pending review."
- The `juno-to-sibyl.md` peer bond includes an unsigned status: `[ ] Sibyl acknowledges signing (pending gestation)`. A bond can be filed and signed by the issuer before the receiving entity is gestated. The bond is active from Juno's side; full bilateral acknowledgment comes when Sibyl's keys exist.
- Bond files are dual-filed: `~/.juno/trust/bonds/` holds the canonical copy; the receiving entity's `trust/bonds/` directory holds a copy. Argus's Day 6 health check caught Chiron's `trust/bonds/` containing only `.gitkeep` — no actual bond files. Salus remediated in commit `99638f1`.

## The Three Bond Types

### `authorized-agent` — koad → Juno

Filed at: `~/.juno/trust/bonds/koad-to-juno.md` + `.md.asc`

This is the root grant. It specifies what Juno can do (file GitHub Issues, issue bonds to team entities, spend up to $50/month, gestate new entities via `koad-io gestate`) and explicitly what she cannot (access koad's personal accounts, sign legal contracts, spend over $50/month, revoke or modify the koad→Juno bond itself, issue authorized-agent bonds to anyone — only koad can do that).

The reporting protocol section is structurally notable: "Juno does not wait for approval on in-scope actions." This is not a policy gate — it is a standing authorization. The bond is the gate. Once signed, Juno acts.

The signing block shows:
```
[x] koad signs this bond with Keybase PGP key (keybase@kingofalldata.com) — 2026-04-02
    Signature: ~/.juno/trust/bonds/koad-to-juno.md.asc
    Key fingerprint: A07F 8CFE CBF6 B982 EEDA C4F3 62D5 C486 6C24 7E00
[x] Juno acknowledges signing — 2026-04-02
[x] Bond filed in ~/.juno/trust/bonds/koad-to-juno.md
[x] Copy filed in ~/.koad-io/trust/
```

Notable: the copy is filed in `~/.koad-io/trust/` — koad's framework directory. Both sides of the trust relationship hold a copy. Neither party's copy is the sole authoritative record.

### `authorized-builder` — Juno → Vulcan

Filed at: `~/.juno/trust/bonds/juno-to-vulcan.md` + `.md.asc`

This is a delegation bond. Juno issues it under the authority she received from koad. The scope is narrower: build assignments come in via GitHub Issues on `koad/vulcan`, Vulcan executes, reports back through the same channel. The workflow protocol section specifies five steps (Assignment → Acknowledgment → Build → Delivery → Verification) — this is the actual Vulcan operating protocol, embedded in the bond.

The signing block shows Vulcan's acknowledgment as unsigned:
```
[x] Juno signs this bond with GPG key (juno@kingofalldata.com) — 2026-04-02
[ ] Vulcan acknowledges signing
```
The bond is active from Juno's side on 2026-04-02. Vulcan's acknowledgment section is pending — this is the normal pattern for a newly gestated entity: the bond precedes full bilateral signing.

The reporting chain is explicit in the bond text:
```
koad (root authority)
  └── Juno (authorized-agent of koad)
        └── Vulcan (authorized-builder of Juno)
```
"Vulcan's actions are scoped by Juno's scope. Juno's scope is scoped by koad's authorization." The chain is traceable from any bond to the root.

### `peer` — Juno → Sibyl

Filed at: `~/.juno/trust/bonds/juno-to-sibyl.md` + `.md.asc`

This is a lateral bond, not a delegation. Peers can file GitHub Issues on each other's repos, reference each other's work, coordinate directly without koad's involvement for routine operations. "Neither entity is subordinate to the other. Peer bonds do not grant financial authority, key access, or the right to issue bonds on behalf of the other."

The signing block shows double-pending:
```
[x] Juno signs this bond with GPG key (juno@kingofalldata.com) — 2026-04-02
[ ] Bond filed in ~/.sibyl/trust/bonds/ (pending entity gestation)
[ ] Sibyl acknowledges signing (pending gestation)
```
Sibyl did not exist as a gestated entity when this bond was filed. The bond was written by Juno, signed by Juno, committed to Juno's repo. Sibyl's side of the dual-file protocol and bilateral acknowledgment are deferred until gestation. The bond's `status` field shows `ACTIVE — signed by Juno 2026-04-02` regardless — the issuing signature is sufficient for Juno's purposes.

## The Dual-File Protocol

Every bond is two files:

1. `koad-to-juno.md` — the human-readable bond document (YAML frontmatter + markdown body)
2. `koad-to-juno.md.asc` — a GPG clearsigned copy of the bond text

The `.asc` file contains the full bond text inside a `-----BEGIN PGP SIGNED MESSAGE-----` / `-----END PGP SIGNATURE-----` envelope. The signature block is appended after `-----BEGIN PGP SIGNATURE-----`. Any verifier can run:

```bash
gpg --verify ~/.juno/trust/bonds/koad-to-juno.md.asc
```

and confirm the text has not been altered since koad signed it, and that it was signed by the key with fingerprint `A07F 8CFE CBF6 B982 EEDA C4F3 62D5 C486 6C24 7E00`.

This is the separation of concerns in the dual-file structure: the `.md` is for human reading, the `.asc` is for machine verification. You can read the bond without the signature; you cannot assert authority without it. An entity presenting an unsigned `.md` has a document. An entity presenting a signed `.md.asc` has a verifiable claim.

## The Cascade Revocation Clause

The root bond makes this explicit:

> "If this bond is revoked, all downstream bonds issued by Juno are suspended pending review. A revocation notice will be filed in `~/.juno/trust/revocation/`."

The juno-to-vulcan bond mirrors this:

> "This bond may be revoked by koad or Juno at any time. Revocation is permanent."

The cascade is one-directional: revoke the root, suspend everything downstream. Revoke a peer bond, and only that lateral relationship is affected — Juno's authorized-agent status from koad and her authorized-builder relationship with Vulcan are untouched.

This means the trust graph has a single point of ultimate authority (koad's root bond) and graceful isolation at the peer layer. Compromising or revoking a peer bond is contained; compromising the root bond requires remediating the entire downstream graph.

## The Signing Key Distinction: Keybase vs. GPG

koad signs with Keybase PGP (`keybase@kingofalldata.com`) — an interactive, authenticated process. Keybase is the human consent gesture: koad had to physically open Keybase and execute `keybase pgp sign` to produce the `.asc`. The Keybase key is tied to koad's Keybase identity, which is separately verified against GitHub, Twitter, and other public accounts. It is not a bureaucratic step — it is the moment at which koad's sovereign intent is cryptographically recorded.

Juno signs with her own GPG key (`juno@kingofalldata.com`) — generated during gestation via `koad-io gestate juno`, stored in `~/.juno/id/`. The Ed25519 key is the entity's identity; the signing is a programmatic act, not an interactive one. Juno can sign bonds autonomously because the koad→Juno root bond already authorizes her to do so.

The asymmetry is architectural: human authority requires human ceremony (Keybase, interactive, tied to real-world identity proofs). Derived authority can operate programmatically (GPG, automated, derived from an existing signed grant). The distinction does not appear in the bond text directly — but it is evident from comparing the signing block of `koad-to-juno.md.asc` (Keybase fingerprint, interactive timestamp) versus `juno-to-vulcan.md.asc` (Juno's GPG fingerprint, same-day automated signing).
