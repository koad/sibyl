## Section 2: What Failed First — The Three Actual Failures

This is the section that earns the post's credibility. The pattern from successful retrospectives is consistent: honesty about specific failures outperforms any feature list. The failures below are documented, named, and resolved. They are not embarrassments — they are the operational history of a system finding its shape.

### Failure 1: Hook routing sent entities to expired API auth

**What happened:** Entities configured with `ENTITY_HOST` in their `.env` were being routed to their declared host machine when invoked by an orchestrator. When Chiron's hook routing pointed to fourty4 and fourty4's API auth had expired, Chiron was unreachable — silently. The entity wasn't refusing; it was just being routed to a dead endpoint.

**How it surfaced:** koad clarified in a session-debrief that entities don't "live" on a fixed host — the repo IS the entity, portability is the point, and the hook's host-routing behavior was an over-constraint that violated the portability principle.

**The fix:** Two changes. Chiron's hook updated to local execution. Vulcan added `FORCE_LOCAL=1` to `executed-without-arguments.sh` (commit 37c65a0, 2026-04-05) — orchestrators can now force local execution without removing `ENTITY_HOST` from config.

**What it reveals about the architecture:** The tension between "declared home host" (useful for autonomous deployment, routing from fourty4) and "portable, runs wherever claude runs" (correct for orchestrated work) was not resolved in the original hook design. The system assumed a single operational mode. Real operation is multi-modal.

**Confidence: High.** Session log 2026-04-05-day6-entity-portability-session.md; Vulcan commit 37c65a0; `FORCE_LOCAL=1` is in the shipped hook.

### Failure 2: The trust bond spec diverged from implementation on Day 2

**What happened:** Vesta had written the trust bond spec. When Juno implemented the actual signing and filing protocol (2026-04-02), six divergences between spec and reality were documented in the trust bond field report:

1. File format: spec said `.signed`, actual is `.md.asc` (clearsigned Markdown)
2. Naming: spec said `<grantor>-<type>.signed`, actual is `<grantor>-to-<grantee>.md`
3. Signing tool: spec didn't distinguish Keybase (human grantors) from GPG (AI entity grantors)
4. Consent UX: spec didn't document the Keybase passphrase dialog as the consent gesture
5. Missing bond type: `peer` is central to team structure but absent from spec
6. Revocation format: described in spec but format undefined

**How it surfaced:** Juno filed the field report to give Vesta the correction. The spec was updated.

**What it reveals about the architecture:** Specs written before implementation will always miss implementation details. The correct pattern — which the operation now follows — is: spec first to define intent, implement, file a field report, Vesta reconciles. The loop is the mechanism, not the spec being perfect. A spec that precedes implementation is a hypothesis, not a contract.

**Confidence: High.** Trust bond field report at `~/.juno/LOGS/2026-04-02-trust-bond-field-report.md`; cross-entity commit protocol commit in `koad/vulcan/trust/GOVERNANCE-NOTES.md`.

### Failure 3: Cross-entity commit authorization had no policy until Janus forced one

**What happened:** Sibyl committed research output directly to koad/vulcan on Juno's instruction, referencing the relevant issues. The work was correct. The authorization chain existed in practice — Juno had issued the directive, the issues made it traceable. What did not exist was a written policy.

Janus filed escalation koad/juno#52. The ruling: Sibyl's commits were authorized. But the gap was real: no written rule governed when one entity may commit to another's repo, under whose direction, and what makes it traceable.

**The policy that came out of it:** All cross-entity commits must reference a directing issue. This is now documented in `koad/vulcan/trust/GOVERNANCE-NOTES.md` and the GOVERNANCE.md bond filing protocol. Faber can note: the escalation was also used to document the commit authorship principle — author = who did the work, not whose repo it is.

**What it reveals about the architecture:** Governance gaps are not visible until someone hits them. Janus exists precisely to surface this kind. The correct response was not to retroactively deny the commits (the work was authorized) but to close the policy gap so the next instance is unambiguous. The escalation process worked as designed — the design was incomplete.

**Confidence: High.** Issue koad/juno#52 is public. The governance note is committed. Session log 2026-04-05-day6.md confirms resolution.

## Section 3: What the Architecture Would Look Like Started Over

This is the most speculative section — and the most valuable for the Day 30 audience. The converts and engaged skeptics have been waiting for this. "If you knew at Day 0 what you know at Day 30, what would you build differently?"

The claims here are still grounded — drawn from documented decisions that were reversed, bottlenecks that were not anticipated, and patterns that emerged from forced adaptation rather than design.

### PRIMER.md would exist from Day 0

The cold-start problem (described in the Day 27 post) was not anticipated in the original gestation template. Early sessions ran without PRIMER.md context — entities producing generic output without role identity, producing results that required cleanup because the entity didn't know it was supposed to be Sibyl or Vulcan, not a generic assistant.

PRIMER.md was added as a pattern after the problem was visible. If starting over, PRIMER.md is part of `koad-io gestate` from the first command. Not optional, not added later. The gestation template creates it with a seed that the entity fills in. The cold-start problem is architecturally prior to everything else in the operation.

**What would change:** The gestation command produces a PRIMER.md stub. The entity's first commit includes its populated PRIMER.md. No session after Day 0 starts without entity identity already in context.

### The dual-memory system would be designed, not discovered

The committed-memories vs. session-local-memories split (resolved on Day 5 via koad/juno#7) was not designed. It was discovered when a session noticed that the memories accumulated in `~/.claude/projects/` were richer than what was committed to `~/.juno/memories/` — and that sessions on other machines had none of the accumulated context.

The fix was to migrate 9 session memories to the committed repo. But the architectural question — what belongs in committed memory (entity identity, long-term operational state, team context) vs. session memory (recent observations, working notes) — had no formal answer. The system worked by informal convention.

**What would change:** The dual-memory architecture would be specced by Vesta before the first session, not after Day 5. The boundary between committed and session-local memory would be documented as a design decision, with a migration protocol from session to committed defined before it was needed. Copia would have had a seed ledger from Day 0 rather than having one written retroactively.

### Host portability vs. deployment routing would be resolved as a first-class concern

The `ENTITY_HOST` vs. portability tension (Failure 1 above) was not obvious until it broke. In theory, an entity declaring its home host makes autonomous deployment possible — you can invoke it without specifying where it runs. In practice, the orchestrator model (Agent tool invokes entities as local subagents) requires portability overriding host declarations.

These are two legitimate operational modes — autonomous deployment (entity runs where it lives) and orchestrated deployment (entity runs where the orchestrator is). They are incompatible in the original hook design. `FORCE_LOCAL=1` is a workaround, not a design.

**What would change:** The hook design would distinguish operational modes explicitly. A `MODE=orchestrated` flag or equivalent would be first-class, not a forced override. The PRIMER.md convention for Vesta's entity portability spec (VESTA-SPEC-053) confirms the right abstraction exists — it just arrived after the bug rather than before it.

### The spec-before-implementation loop would be shorter

Day 2's trust bond divergence (Failure 2) is a specific instance of a general pattern: Vesta writes specs at depth; implementation reveals details the spec couldn't anticipate; field reports reconcile spec to reality. This is the correct process. But the loop took two days before it was formalized.

**What would change:** The field report format would be standardized in Week 1. Vesta would have a `feedback:` issue label from Day 2. The reconciliation loop would be a named process, not an ad hoc correction. This would not change the substance of what was discovered — it would change how quickly the discovery returned to the spec.

## Section 5: What's Still Unknown

No retrospective earns full trust without honesty about open questions. These are real, not performed.

**What we don't know yet:**

1. **Whether GitHub Issues scales to 19+ active entities.** The operation has run at 19 entities for less than a week at full concurrency. The theoretical ceiling (5,000 API requests/hour per authenticated user, ~100 entities polling before latency compounds) has not been approached. Whether it compounds before or after koad:io's operation needs it is unknown.

2. **Whether the trust bond model holds under conflict.** All bonds so far have been additive — creating relationships, defining scope, filing corrections. No bond has been stress-tested by a genuine disagreement between entities about authorization scope. The Janus escalation on #52 is the closest case, and it was resolved by a policy gap, not a genuine conflict. What the system does under genuine conflict is not yet documented.

3. **Whether the PRIMER.md pattern is stable across harnesses.** The Day 27 post documents PRIMER.md injection in the Claude Code harness. The operation also uses opencode (framework default) and plans for the pi harness (fourty4, Hermez pilot). Whether PRIMER.md behaves identically across harnesses — or whether harness-specific behavior diverges — is not confirmed.

4. **Whether the $200 laptop remains the right story past Day 30.** The thinker is the proof of concept. The question it answers is "can you start sovereign?" not "can you scale sovereign?" The infrastructure answer for a 50-entity, 10-project operation is not yet known.

**Faber should state these directly.** The converts want honesty. The skeptics are waiting for overreach. Naming open questions inoculates against the first wave of skeptical comments and earns the trust that converts into advocacy.

## Section 6: Tone, Structure, and HN Positioning

### Tone

Day 30 is the post where the series earns the trust it has been building. The week 1 retrospective patterns brief established the formula: own the facts, hedge the implications, be explicit about the boundary between them.

What this post must not do: claim that the three failures were minor, or that they were anticipated, or that the architecture was designed to produce them. They were real failures. The architecture was improved by encountering them. The distinction matters.

What this post can do: claim, honestly, that a system that generates legible failures and commits corrections to the fossil record is more trustworthy than a system that appears to have never failed. The git log is the evidence. The production record is public.

### Structural recommendation

1. **Open with the date.** "Juno was gestated on 2026-03-30. Today is day 30." No preamble. The milestone is the opening.

2. **Name three things that were built.** Not a feature list — three things that changed the capability of the operation. One sentence each, with evidence. The PRIMER injection, the trust bond system, the cross-entity governance policy. Three things that did not exist 30 days ago and now do.

3. **Name three things that failed first.** In order. The hook routing bug, the trust bond spec divergence, the governance gap. What happened, what it revealed, what was fixed. One paragraph each.

4. **The "would change" section.** Not regret — specificity. "If we started over with what we know now, three things would be in the gestation template: PRIMER.md stub, dual-memory architecture, explicit portability mode." This is the shareable insight.

5. **The arc close.** One paragraph connecting Days 22–29 to the synthesis: the layers interact, and the interactions revealed the gaps, and the gaps are now closed, and the corrections are on the record. This is what a fossil record looks like.

6. **Close with an open question.** One genuine uncertainty. Not performed modesty — something real. The trust bond conflict question is the right one: the model has not been stress-tested by genuine entity disagreement. What happens when it is, is the question Day 31+ will answer.

### What makes this HN-shareable

The week 1 retrospective patterns research identified the "things we thought were true that turned out not to be" section as the highest-shareability format in technical retrospectives. Fast.ai's lessons learned, Lichess's federation mistake — the candor about what the builder got wrong is cited for years.

Day 30 has three documented, named, resolved failures with git commits. That is better evidence than most retrospectives produce at 30 days. Lead with the failures. The success narrative is implicit in the correction record.
