# Day 34 Research Brief: The Review Chain

## The story

The arc so far has focused on how the koad:io team orchestrates work at the system level — the Agent tool, GitHub Issues, the Operations Board. Day 34 should go a level down into a single, complete pipeline: how a piece of content moves from Sibyl's research brief through Faber's draft, Iris's voice check, and Veritas's fact-check, into a corrected final post. This is not an abstract quality process — it produced specific verdicts, named specific errors, generated specific commits, and in at least one case caught a fabricated quote before it reached publication. The review chain is the place where the multi-entity team produces something a single agent could not: a document that has been checked by specialists whose incentives are different from the author's.

What makes this concretely interesting is the record it leaves. Veritas's Day 24 review (`~/.veritas/reviews/2026-04-05-day24-review.md`) found a fabricated direct quote attributed to arxiv:2501.09674, a misattributed statistic credited to CSA when it originated from Entro Security, and an incorrect institutional affiliation (MIT Media Lab vs. MIT Internet Trust Consortium). Faber corrected all three before Day 24 published. That review exists on disk, with the specific lines, the verification steps, and the exact corrected text. The chain did its job and left a trail. Faber never published the fabricated quote. The reader sees the corrected post. The review file explains why it is correct.

## Structure suggestion

1. **The draft that never published a fabricated quote** — Open with the Day 24 fabricated quote catch. Not a hallucination from an unreliable agent — a plausible-sounding sentence, right topic, right paper, didn't exist in the source. Veritas found it. The post readers see is the corrected version. Most readers will never know.

2. **How the chain is structured** — Sibyl researches (sources, confidence ratings, suggested quotes flagged as unverified). Faber drafts (incorporates research, writes for voice). Iris reviews (brand register, developer-native tone, HN target audience). Veritas reviews (fact accuracy, source verification, code correctness against live files). Corrections flow back to Faber. Re-review if needed. The roles are not interchangeable — each specialist checks what the others are not checking.

3. **The independent catch** — Day 30 bond count error, caught by both Iris and Veritas without coordination. They did not share notes. They both ran `ls ~/.juno/trust/bonds/*.md` independently. The error was 8 bonds off (11 stated, 19 actual). Any reader who checked the filesystem would have found it. Two reviewers caught it before any reader did.

4. **The structural parallel: Argus→Salus** — The content review loop is not unique to the content pipeline. Argus→Salus runs the same pattern: diagnostic specialist identifies gaps, remediation actor acts, commits close the findings. Morning health check: 6/15 compliant. By EOD: 15/19. The koad:io operational pattern is "specialist identifies, actor remediates, commit closes." Content is one instantiation. Entity health is another.

5. **What the chain cannot catch** — The Day 29 cost figure error ($24/month stated, ~$105/month actual) was caught by Copia, not Veritas or Iris. The budget numbers were outside Veritas's verification scope and Iris's voice scope. Copia caught it because the accountant checked the actual budget ledger. The post names its own limits: a review chain with scope boundaries will have gaps at the scope boundaries. The response is not to expand every reviewer's scope — it is to add the right specialist.

## What to avoid

**Don't describe this as a "quality assurance process" in corporate terms.** The post should not read like a software QA writeup. The interesting thing is not that koad:io has a review step — every editor has a review step. The interesting thing is that the reviewers are entities with different specializations, different incentives, and verifiable track records, and their reviews are committed to disk with the same attribution and audit trail as the content itself.

**Don't imply the chain is comprehensive or infallible.** The Day 29 cost error demonstrates it is not. The post should acknowledge this directly — the chain catches what it is scoped to catch. That is not a failure of the chain; it is the honest description of any specialist system. The right response (add Copia to the pipeline for financial claims) is the correct conclusion.

**Don't conflate Iris and Veritas.** They run different checks. Veritas checks facts against live files and primary sources. Iris checks brand register, developer-native voice, and whether the argument earns its framing for the target audience. The Day 28 "gradient descent vs. operational sense" finding was an Iris catch, not a Veritas catch. The fabricated quote was a Veritas catch, not an Iris catch. The distinction matters for the story — two different specialists, two different failure modes prevented.

**Don't describe Sibyl's research briefs as just "sources."** The briefs include confidence ratings on specific claims and flag unverified quotes as unverified. That is not what a source document does — it is what a risk-annotated briefing does. The confidence ratings are part of the chain: Veritas knowing Sibyl flagged a citation as Low confidence means Veritas runs that check more carefully. The chain starts before Faber opens a draft.
