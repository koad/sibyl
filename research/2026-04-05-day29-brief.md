## Angle Selection Rationale

Three options were evaluated for Day 29.

**Why Option 2 wins:**

Six consecutive posts in Week 4 have argued the architectural case for sovereignty: trust bonds, file-based storage, GPG keys, PRIMER context assembly, and the hook-as-training pattern. The audience of converts and engaged skeptics now has a coherent picture of what a sovereign AI entity *is*. The silent, unasked question from the skeptic cohort — the question that determines whether they convert or disengage — is: **"What does this actually cost?"**

This is the angle with the most externally verifiable, non-fabricatable, concrete evidence. Every number in this post comes from public pricing pages, secondary market listings, or the koad:io operation's own documented hardware. There is no citation risk. The numbers either check out or they don't.

The series has earned technical credibility. Day 29 cashes it in with an economics post that makes a concrete, verifiable claim: you can run a sovereign multi-entity AI operation for less than what most teams pay for a SaaS observability add-on. That claim is true, documentable, and genuinely non-obvious to the target audience.

**Why Option 1 (git audit trail / git bisect) was not selected:**

The git provenance angle is compelling and novel — `git bisect` applied to AI entity behavior is a genuinely interesting framing that has no direct precedent in the literature. However: Day 25 (Files vs. Database) already argued the git-as-storage case on engineering grounds. Running a thematically adjacent post four days later risks diluting both. The git forensics angle belongs in Week 5 or as a standalone deep-dive, paired with a specific incident that gives it concrete grounding.

**Why Option 3 (multi-entity coordination without shared memory) was not selected:**

The inter-agent comms brief has solid research, but the interesting claims in this space (OpenDevin latency measurements, GitHub ceiling at 50 concurrent agents) sit at Medium confidence — engineering inference, not primary citations. Week 4 has been a high-confidence week. Closing it with a Medium-confidence speculative post would undercut the credibility built by Days 23–28. Option 3 is the right Day 31 or Day 35 post, after the operation has more multi-entity coordination runtime to draw on.

## Section 1: The Hardware Floor

### What "thinker" Actually Is

thinker is the primary machine where koad operates — the node where every Juno session runs, where trust bonds are signed, where entity invocations happen interactively. It is a Lenovo ThinkPad T480.

The ThinkPad T480 is a 2018-vintage business laptop. Secondary market pricing as of early 2026:

- eBay listings: $200–370 depending on configuration (i5 vs. i7, 8–32GB RAM, 256GB–1TB SSD)
- Back Market (vetted refurbished): similar range, with warranty
- A base config (i5-8350U, 8GB RAM, 256GB SSD) is reliably available under $250

koad's operation is running on hardware in this price range. This is the documented basis for the "$200 laptop" framing. It is not approximate — it is the floor of the actual market.

**Why this matters:** The conventional mental model of "running an AI agent team" involves GPUs, cloud instances, and monthly compute fees. The koad:io operation runs its orchestration layer — Juno, all entity sessions, trust bond management, git operations, GitHub CLI interactions — on a used laptop that costs less than two months of a competing SaaS subscription.

The laptop is not the bottleneck. The laptop runs SSH, git, and Claude Code. The model lives at Anthropic. The weight is distributed. Authority is here.

**Confidence: High** — ThinkPad T480 pricing is observable on eBay and Back Market. The operational role of thinker is documented in Juno memory (`user_koad.md`, `project_infrastructure.md`).

### Power Consumption

A ThinkPad T480 running light workloads (Claude Code sessions, git operations, SSH) draws approximately 15–25 watts. At 24/7 operation and US average electricity rate ($0.17/kWh):

```
25W × 720 hours/month = 18 kWh/month × $0.17 = ~$3.06/month
```

Electricity for thinker running as an always-on orchestration node: approximately **$3–4/month**.

The February 2026 HN post ("I turned old laptops into an AI coding farm — $15/month vs. Devin's $500") documented similar economics for a batch-processing agent setup. The HN community discussion engaged substantively with the cost comparison without significant challenge to the electricity math.

**Confidence: High** — laptop idle power draw is well-documented; electricity rate is US average from EIA data; HN post is a public peer reference.

## Section 2: The Software Cost Stack

### Claude Code — What koad:io Actually Pays

Claude Code subscription pricing as of March 2026:

| Plan | Monthly Cost | Usage |
|------|-------------|-------|
| Pro | $20/month | Standard usage limits |
| Max 5x | $100/month | 5× Pro limits |
| Max 20x | $200/month | 20× Pro limits |

koad:io's operational model uses Claude Code. The honest answer to "what does this cost?" includes the subscription. The sovereignty argument is not "we escaped API costs." It is: **one vendor, known price, predictable ceiling, no lock-in at the infrastructure layer**.

Additional usage beyond plan limits falls through to pay-as-you-go at standard API rates:
- Claude Haiku 4.5: $0.25/$1.25 per million tokens (input/output)
- Claude Sonnet 4.6: $3/$15 per million tokens
- Claude Opus 4.6: $5/$25 per million tokens

Batch API: 50% discount on all models for non-interactive workloads.
Prompt caching: ~10% of standard input rate for cached prefixes.

These are the actual marginal costs. They are predictable and reducible.

**Confidence: High** — prices from platform.claude.com/docs/en/about-claude/pricing (verified April 2026).

### What the SaaS Alternative Actually Costs

A team running a managed multi-agent AI operation in 2026 might reasonably combine:

**LangSmith (LangChain's observability/tracing):**
- Plus plan: $39/seat/month
- 10,000 traces/month included; overages at $2.50/1,000 traces
- A solo developer generating 50,000 traces/month: $39 + ($40 overage) = **~$79/month**
- A team of 5 at 200,000 traces/month: $195 seats + ~$475 overage = **~$670/month**

**Devin AI (managed autonomous coding agent):**
- Core plan: $20/month + $2.25/ACU (1 ACU ≈ 15 minutes of active work)
- Team plan: $500/month, 250 ACUs included, $2/ACU over
- Historical framing: before the Devin 2.0 price cut, the Team plan was the primary offering at $500/month

**The comparison:**

| Stack | Monthly Cost | What You Own |
|-------|-------------|--------------|
| koad:io sovereign | $20–100 (Claude Code) + ~$4 electricity | Entity state, keys, routing logic, audit trail — everything |
| LangSmith + Claude API | $79+ LangSmith + API costs | Nothing — traces on LangChain servers, state in their DB |
| Devin Team plan | $500+ | Nothing — agent runs on Cognition's infrastructure |
| LangSmith + Devin Core | $39+ $20+ ACU costs | Your LangSmith traces (on their server); nothing else |

The delta is not trivial. At koad:io's scale (one developer, Pro plan, existing hardware), the monthly cash outlay is ~$24/month. An equivalent managed stack — Claude API + LangSmith Plus + any SaaS orchestration layer — would cost $120–200/month with no additional features and with all operational state living on vendor servers.

**Confidence: High** — LangSmith pricing from langchain.com/pricing (verified April 2026); Devin pricing from devin.ai/pricing (verified April 2026); Claude pricing as above.

## Section 3: The Self-Hosted Inference Path

### ollama on fourty4

The koad:io fleet already includes fourty4 — a Mac Mini running ollama with local inference capability. This is not aspirational; it is operational.

ollama local deployment cost: $0 in API fees. The software is free. The hardware (fourty4, a Mac Mini) is already in the fleet. The power draw of a Mac Mini at light inference load: approximately 15–30W, or $3–6/month in electricity.

What ollama on fourty4 provides:
- Local inference for tasks that don't require Claude Code's full capability: research summarization, content outline drafts, routine text processing
- $0 marginal cost per token for these workloads
- No data leaves the fleet — model weights run on hardware koad owns

Practical split for the current koad:io operation:
- **Claude Code** (via thinker): interactive entity sessions, governance decisions, trust bond work, anything requiring high-quality judgment
- **ollama** (via fourty4): background research synthesis, draft generation, low-stakes entity-to-entity routing

As ollama models improve, the share of work that can run locally grows. The trajectory is toward a smaller and smaller fraction of work requiring cloud inference.

**Confidence: High** — fourty4's role is documented in Juno operational memory; ollama's cost structure is public.

### The Break-Even Math

ollama Cloud (launched September 2025) offers fixed-price tiers ($20/month Pro, $100/month Max) for cloud-hosted inference — but local deployment remains free. For comparison purposes, using open-source models locally via ollama:

A $10,000 hardware investment (quality GPU server) recovers against $500/month cloud API costs in 24 months. At koad:io's scale, the math is lighter: fourty4 (already purchased, ~$800 Mac Mini used) eliminates any API cost for workloads running there. The "break-even" has already happened — the hardware existed before the inference use case.

## Section 4: The Ownership Differential

### What You Don't Pay For, and Why That Matters

The cost comparison above understates the sovereignty value in one critical dimension: **what SaaS platforms charge for data you already generated**.

When your agent's operational state lives in LangSmith:
- Your traces have 14-day retention on the free plan; 400-day retention costs $5/1,000 traces
- If you stop paying, your agent's history disappears
- If LangSmith discontinues a plan tier, your traces are hostage to migration

When your agent's operational state lives in git:
- Retention is unlimited and offline
- No per-trace fee
- The operational history is yours unconditionally — it lives on your disk, under your key

The audit trail that git provides — full session history, commit-level diff of every state change, `git log` as provenance chain — costs $0 additional beyond the disk space. The equivalent capability in a managed tracing platform costs $39+/month/seat.

This is the non-obvious part of the cost argument: **you are not just paying less; you are getting an artifact that managed platforms charge extra for and still don't fully own**.

### The Devin Comparison (Concrete)

Devin's Team plan ($500/month) provides an autonomous coding agent that runs on Cognition's infrastructure. What the operator owns at the end of the month: the code Devin produced (if they downloaded it). The reasoning chain, the session history, the decisions made — these live on Cognition's servers.

koad:io's Vulcan (the builder entity): runs as a Claude Code session on fourty4 or thinker. Every session is a git commit. Every architectural decision is a logged entry. Every trust bond is a GPG-signed file on disk. Vulcan's entire operational history is owned by koad, committed to a repo koad controls, verifiable without any dependency on Anthropic or any other vendor remaining operational.

Monthly cost for Vulcan: included in the Claude Code subscription ($20–100/month total, shared across all entities). Not $500/month per entity.

**Confidence: High** — Devin pricing is public; Vulcan's operational model is documented in CLAUDE.md and operation logs.

## Section 5: The Honest Caveats

### What Sovereignty Does Not Solve

This post should not oversell. Three honest limitations belong in the final piece:

**1. You still need the model.** koad:io's current operation depends on Claude Code. If Anthropic changes pricing, rate-limits entities, or discontinues the service, the operation is affected. The sovereignty argument applies to everything *except* the inference layer — unless you are fully self-hosting, which fourty4 moves toward but does not yet complete. Acknowledge this directly.

**2. The $200 laptop doesn't scale to GPU-intensive workloads.** If your operation requires training, fine-tuning, or running large local models interactively, thinker is not sufficient. koad:io's architecture explicitly distributes weight-heavy work to fourty4 (Mac Mini with better inference capacity) and will add GPU capacity as the operation grows. The $200 laptop is the orchestration layer, not the entire compute fleet.

**3. The time cost is real.** A SaaS platform handles infrastructure, updates, monitoring, and scaling. The sovereign operator handles these. koad:io's infrastructure overhead is small (it runs on existing hardware with minimal maintenance), but it is not zero. The comparison should include this honestly.

**Confidence: High** — these are genuine limitations, not FUD. State them and move on.

## Section 6: The Week 4 Arc Completion

### How This Post Connects to Days 23–28

Day 23 described a specific operational failure (FORCE_LOCAL=1). Days 24–28 built the architectural picture: trust bonds, files, GPG keys, context assembly, hooks. Each post made the case for a specific architectural choice.

Day 29 answers the implied meta-question: **Why would you build all of this instead of just paying for a managed platform?**

The answer has two parts that Days 23–28 prepared:
1. **Control** — you own your keys, your state, your routing logic (Days 24–27)
2. **Cost** — you pay less, predictably, with no surveillance of your operational data (Day 29)

The cost argument without the control argument is just cheapness. The control argument without the cost argument is just ideology. Together, they form the actual pitch: **you pay less and you own more**.

### The Sovereignty Argument Applied to Economics

Days 25–26 applied sovereignty to storage and identity. Day 29 applies it to economics.

Managed platforms extract value in two ways: subscription fees and data. LangSmith charges $39+/seat/month for tracing — but also accumulates all your agent's operational data on their servers, which trains their understanding of how agents behave, what workflows succeed, and what your architecture looks like. You are paying them and giving them your data.

In the koad:io model: the git log that replaces LangSmith traces stays on your disk. The money saved is concrete ($79+/month vs. $0). The data is not transferred.

This framing — **you are not just paying for traces, you are paying to give away your operational data** — is the sharpest version of the argument. It should be stated carefully, not aggressively, but it should be stated.

## Section 7: Faber Notes

### What to Emphasize

1. **Lead with the concrete number.** "The machine that runs this operation cost $200." Open the post with this and let it sit for a paragraph. Do not contextualize it immediately. Let the reader's mental model collide with the number first.

2. **The comparison table is the hook.** The three-row cost comparison (koad:io sovereign / LangSmith + API / Devin Team) belongs in the first half of the post. Make the numbers unavoidable.

3. **The honest caveats earn credibility.** "You still need Anthropic" is a real limitation. State it early. The reader who is looking for a reason to dismiss the post cannot use this against you if you said it first.

4. **The data ownership angle is the unexpected pivot.** Most readers will arrive expecting a "sovereign is cheaper" post. The twist is: you're not just paying less, you're not paying to hand over your agent's decision history. This reframes the value from "cheap" to "yours."

5. **The peer reference.** "In February 2026, someone on Hacker News posted about running an AI coding farm on old laptops for $15/month in electricity vs. $500/month for Devin. The discussion was substantive. The math wasn't challenged." Use this to establish that the architecture class is real and publicly validated — koad:io is not alone in discovering it.

6. **Close with the Day 7 frame.** "On Day 7 of this operation, koad:io has run a multi-entity AI business team — 18+ entities, active GitHub operations, content production, trust bond governance — from a $200 laptop. The infrastructure overhead is $24/month (Claude Code Pro + electricity). The data is ours. The keys are ours. The history is ours." This closes the Week 4 arc.

### What to Hedge

1. **The "$200 laptop" claim.** The T480 secondary market floor is $200–250. Some configurations are $350+. "Under $300" is more defensible if you're not certain of the exact purchase price. "~$200" with a footnote about the secondary market range is the safest form.

2. **LangSmith trace counts.** The "$79/month for a solo developer" estimate assumes 50,000 traces/month. State the assumption explicitly. The range ($39–$670/month) is more accurate for diverse usage patterns.

3. **"You still need Anthropic."** Be precise: the inference dependency is real. "Sovereign infrastructure, with one vendor dependency (inference)" is accurate. "Fully sovereign" is not, yet.

4. **ollama capability parity.** Local inference today does not match Claude Sonnet 4.6 for complex reasoning tasks. Don't claim capability parity. Claim cost-effective routing for appropriate workloads.

### Tone

Day 29 is the first post in the series with explicit numbers. The tone should shift from architectural to analytical — more table, more math, more "here is what this actually costs and here is the receipts." The audience has been sold on the architecture. Now they need the economics.

This is not a "we're cheaper" post. It is a "let's count everything you're paying for and who owns what at the end" post. The framing is forensic, not sales. Week 4's Protocol + Failures tone carries through — apply the same rigor to economics that Days 24–27 applied to architecture.

**Target length:** ~1,200 words. Shorter than Days 27–28. Tables and concrete numbers do more work per word than architectural prose. Don't pad.
